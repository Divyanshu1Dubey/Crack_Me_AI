# Ophthalmology — UPSC CMS Comprehensive Notes

## Anatomy of the Eye

### Layers of Eyeball
1. **Fibrous coat**: Sclera (posterior 5/6) + Cornea (anterior 1/6)
2. **Vascular coat (Uvea)**: Choroid + Ciliary body + Iris
3. **Nervous coat**: Retina (10 layers, photoreceptors: rods and cones)

### Cornea
- **Layers** (5): Epithelium → Bowman's membrane → Stroma (90%) → Descemet's membrane → Endothelium
- **Avascular** — nourished by aqueous humor and tears
- **Transparency** maintained by: Regular arrangement of stromal fibers, relative dehydration (endothelial pump)
- **Refractive power**: 43 D (2/3 of total eye power)

### Lens
- **Biconvex, avascular, transparent** — derived from ectoderm
- **Refractive power**: 15-20 D (1/3 of total)
- **Accommodation**: Ciliary muscle contraction → zonules relax → lens becomes more convex
- **Presbyopia**: Age-related loss of accommodation (starts ~40 years)

### Aqueous Humor
- **Produced by**: Ciliary body (2-3 μL/min)
- **Pathway**: Posterior chamber → Pupil → Anterior chamber → Trabecular meshwork → Canal of Schlemm → Episcleral veins
- **Intraocular pressure (IOP)**: Normal 10-21 mmHg
- **Measured by**: Goldmann applanation tonometry (gold standard), Schiotz tonometry (indentation)

## Refractive Errors

### Myopia (Short-sightedness)
- **Image formed**: In front of retina (axial length too long or cornea too curved)
- **Correction**: Concave (minus) lens
- **High myopia** (>6D): Risk of retinal detachment, macular degeneration, glaucoma

### Hypermetropia (Far-sightedness)
- **Image formed**: Behind retina (axial length too short)
- **Correction**: Convex (plus) lens
- **May present** with convergent squint in children (accommodative esotropia)

### Astigmatism
- **Unequal curvature** of cornea in different meridians
- **Correction**: Cylindrical lens
- **Types**: Regular (with-the-rule, against-the-rule) and Irregular (keratoconus, corneal scars)

### Presbyopia
- **Age-related loss of accommodation** — difficulty reading close objects
- **Onset**: ~40 years (requires +1D reading glasses, increases ~+0.5D per 5 years)
- **Correction**: Convex (reading) glasses, bifocals, progressive lenses

## Glaucoma

### Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma (POAG)
- **Most common type** of glaucoma
- **Open angles**, but reduced outflow through trabecular meshwork
- **Insidious onset**: Painless, gradual visual field loss (nasal step → arcuate scotoma → tunnel vision)
- **Risk factors**: Age >40, family history, myopia, diabetes, thin CCT
- **Diagnosis**: ↑IOP, optic disc cupping (C:D ratio >0.6), visual field defects (Humphrey perimetry)
- **Treatment**: Prostaglandin analogs (latanoprost — first-line), beta-blockers (timolol), trabeculectomy

### Primary Angle-Closure Glaucoma (PACG)
- **Acute presentation**: Severe eye pain, headache, nausea, vomiting, colored halos, red eye
- **Shallow anterior chamber**, mid-dilated fixed pupil, corneal edema, very high IOP (>50 mmHg)
- **Risk factors**: Hypermetropia, age, female sex, Asian ethnicity
- **Emergency treatment**: IV mannitol, pilocarpine, timolol, acetazolamide
- **Definitive treatment**: Laser peripheral iridotomy (both eyes)

### Secondary Glaucoma
- **Neovascular glaucoma**: Complication of diabetic retinopathy, CRVO
- **Phacolytic glaucoma**: Mature/hypermature cataract — lens proteins clog trabecular meshwork
- **Steroid-induced glaucoma**: Topical/systemic steroids — mimics POAG

## Cataract

### Classification
- **Senile cataract** (most common): Nuclear, cortical, posterior subcapsular
- **Congenital**: TORCH infections, galactosemia, Down syndrome
- **Metabolic**: Diabetes (snowflake cataract), galactosemia (oil droplet), hypoparathyroidism
- **Traumatic**: Rosette-shaped (contusion), Vossius ring (imprint of iris pigment)
- **Drug-induced**: Steroids (posterior subcapsular), chlorpromazine (anterior subcapsular)

### Stages of Senile Cataract
1. **Immature**: Partially opaque, iris shadow present
2. **Mature**: Completely opaque, no iris shadow, pearly white
3. **Hypermature** (Morgagnian): Liquefied cortex, lens nucleus sinks
4. **Complications**: Phacolytic glaucoma, phacoanaphylactic uveitis

### Treatment
- **Indications**: Visual impairment affecting daily activities
- **Surgery**: Phacoemulsification + posterior chamber IOL (gold standard)
  - SICS (Small Incision Cataract Surgery): Alternative in developing countries
  - ECCE: Older technique, larger wound
  - ICCE: Historical — entire lens with capsule removed
- **IOL power calculation**: SRB formula, SRK-T formula — based on axial length and keratometry

## Retinal Disorders

### Diabetic Retinopathy
- **Most common cause of blindness** in working-age adults
- **Non-proliferative (NPDR)**:
  - Microaneurysms (earliest sign), dot-blot hemorrhages, hard exudates, cotton-wool spots
  - CSME (Clinically Significant Macular Edema): Treated with anti-VEGF (bevacizumab, ranibizumab)
- **Proliferative (PDR)**:
  - Neovascularization of disc (NVD) or elsewhere (NVE)
  - Complications: Vitreous hemorrhage, tractional retinal detachment
  - Treatment: Pan-retinal photocoagulation (PRP), anti-VEGF, vitrectomy

### Retinal Detachment
- **Rhegmatogenous**: Most common — retinal break/hole → fluid seeps under retina
  - Risk factors: High myopia, trauma, aphakia, lattice degeneration
  - Symptoms: Flashes (photopsia), floaters, curtain/shadow over vision
  - Treatment: Scleral buckle, pneumatic retinopexy, vitrectomy
- **Tractional**: Fibrous bands pull retina — diabetic retinopathy, sickle cell
- **Exudative**: Fluid accumulation without break — tumors, inflammation, Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada

### Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO)
- **Sudden, painless loss of vision**
- **Fundus**: Pale retina with cherry-red spot at macula (choroidal blood supply visible through thin fovea)
- **Association**: Carotid artery atherosclerosis, giant cell arteritis, cardiac emboli
- **Treatment**: Ocular massage, anterior chamber paracentesis (emergency — <90 min window)
- **RAPD** (relative afferent pupillary defect) present

### Central Retinal Vein Occlusion (CRVO)
- **Fundus**: "Blood and thunder" — widespread hemorrhages, dilated tortuous veins, disc edema
- **Risk factors**: Hypertension, diabetes, glaucoma
- **Complications**: Neovascular glaucoma, macular edema
- **Treatment**: Anti-VEGF for macular edema, PRP for neovascularization

### Age-Related Macular Degeneration (ARMD)
- **Leading cause of blindness** in elderly (developed countries)
- **Dry (atrophic)**: Drusen, geographic atrophy — 90% cases, slow progression — no definitive treatment (AREDS supplements)
- **Wet (neovascular)**: Choroidal neovascularization — 10% cases, rapid loss — anti-VEGF injections

## Corneal Disorders

### Corneal Ulcer
- **Bacterial**: S. aureus, Pseudomonas (contact lens users — rapid, greenish discharge)
- **Fungal**: Aspergillus, Fusarium — feathery margins, satellite lesions, trauma with vegetable matter
- **Viral (Herpes simplex)**: Dendritic ulcer — fluorescein staining shows branching pattern
  - Treatment: Topical acyclovir — **DO NOT use steroids** (worsen herpes keratitis)
- **Acanthamoeba**: Contact lens users — ring infiltrate, severe pain disproportionate to findings

### Keratoconus
- **Progressive thinning and protrusion** of cornea (cone shape)
- **Signs**: Munson's sign (V-shaped lower lid on downgaze), Fleischer ring (iron deposit), Vogt striae
- **Associations**: Atopy, Down syndrome, Ehlers-Danlos, contact lens wear
- **Treatment**: Rigid contact lenses → Collagen cross-linking (CXL) → Keratoplasty

## Squint (Strabismus)

### Types
- **Esotropia** (convergent): Eye turns inward — most common in children
  - Accommodative esotropia: Due to uncorrected hypermetropia — treat with glasses
- **Exotropia** (divergent): Eye turns outward — intermittent exotropia most common
- **Paralytic squint**: Due to cranial nerve palsy (III, IV, VI)

### Amblyopia (Lazy Eye)
- **Reduced vision in one eye** due to abnormal visual development — no organic cause
- **Critical period**: First 6-8 years — must treat early
- **Treatment**: Patching (occlusion) of good eye, atropine penalization, correct refractive error
- **Most common cause of monocular vision loss** in children

## Eye Emergencies

### Chemical Burns
- **Alkali burns** more dangerous than acid (deeper penetration — liquefactive necrosis)
- **Immediate treatment**: Copious irrigation with water/saline for at least 30 minutes
- **Check pH** — continue irrigation until pH 7.0-7.4
- **Roper-Hall classification**: Grades I-IV based on corneal haze and limbal ischemia

### Acute Congestive Glaucoma
- See angle-closure glaucoma above — ophthalmic emergency

### Orbital Cellulitis
- **Pre-septal** (periorbital): Anterior to orbital septum — common in children, mild
- **Post-septal** (orbital): True emergency — proptosis, ophthalmoplegia, ↓vision
  - CT scan mandatory — look for subperiosteal abscess
  - IV antibiotics + surgical drainage if abscess present
  - Complication: Cavernous sinus thrombosis

## Lid Disorders

### Chalazion vs Stye (Hordeolum)
- **Chalazion**: Chronic granuloma of Meibomian gland — painless, points toward conjunctiva
  - Treatment: Warm compress, I&C (incision and curettage) from conjunctival side
- **External hordeolum (stye)**: Acute infection of Zeis/Moll glands — painful, points to skin
- **Internal hordeolum**: Acute infection of Meibomian gland

### Ptosis
- **Congenital**: Dystrophy of levator palpebrae superioris — unilateral, marcus gunn jaw-winking
- **Acquired**: CN III palsy, Horner syndrome, myasthenia gravis, aponeurotic (senile)
- **Horner syndrome**: Miosis + ptosis (partial) + anhidrosis + enophthalmos
  - Causes: Pancoast tumor, carotid dissection, brainstem stroke
- **Treatment**: Frontalis sling (poor LPS function), levator resection (good function)

# Forensic Medicine — UPSC CMS Comprehensive Notes (Extended)

## Forensic Thanatology (Death and After)

### Definition of Death
- **Somatic death**: Irreversible cessation of vital functions (brain, heart, lungs)
- **Brainstem death**: Irreversible loss of brainstem function — legal definition in India
  - Tests: Fixed dilated pupils, absent corneal reflex, absent vestibulo-ocular reflex, absent gag/cough, no spontaneous respiration (apnea test)
  - Certified by panel of 4 doctors (including one from hospital and treating doctor)
- **Cellular/Molecular death**: Death of individual cells after somatic death (hours to days)

### Post-mortem Changes

#### Early Changes
- **Cooling (Algor mortis)**: Body cools to ambient temperature
  - Rate: ~1.5°F (0.8°C) per hour initially (varies with environment, clothing, body habitus)
  - **Henssge nomogram**: Used for time since death estimation
- **Rigor mortis**: Stiffening due to ATP depletion → actin-myosin cross-bridges persist
  - **Nysten's rule**: Starts in eyelids/jaw → neck → upper limbs → lower limbs (2-4 hours)
  - Fully established: 12 hours | Disappears: 24-48 hours (decomposition breaks bonds)
  - **Cadaveric spasm (instantaneous rigor)**: Immediate rigor in a muscle group — preserves last action
  - **Heat rigor**: Coagulation of proteins (not true rigor) — pugilistic attitude in burns
- **Livor mortis (Hypostasis)**: Gravitational pooling of blood in dependent parts
  - Appears: 30 min - 2 hours | Fixed: 6-8 hours (hemoglobin diffuses into tissues)
  - Color: Bluish-purple | **CO poisoning**: Cherry pink | **Cyanide**: Brick red
  - Absent at pressure points (contact with surface)

#### Late Changes
- **Decomposition**: Greenish discoloration starts in right iliac fossa (cecum — bacterial gas)
  - Marbling → bloating → tissue liquefaction → skeletonization
- **Adipocere**: Saponification of body fat in moist/anaerobic environment — waxy, yellowish
  - Preserves body shape — useful for identification weeks-months after death
- **Mummification**: Dry, hot environment — desiccation preserves features
- **Maceration**: Intrauterine fetal death — softening, peeling of skin in amniotic fluid

### Time Since Death Estimation
| Finding | Approximate Time |
|---------|-----------------|
| Body still warm, no rigor | < 2 hours |
| Body cooling, rigor developing | 2-6 hours |
| Rigor fully established | 6-12 hours |
| Rigor passing off | 24-48 hours |
| Decomposition starts | 48-72 hours |
| Maggots present | Days-weeks |
| Skeletonization | Months-years |

## Identification

### Methods of Identification
1. **Physical features**: Sex, age, height, build, skin color, scars, tattoos, moles
2. **Dental identification**: Most reliable in mass disasters (dental records survive)
3. **Fingerprints (Dactylography)**: Types — loops (65%, most common), whorls, arches, composites
4. **DNA profiling**: Most reliable method — STR analysis
5. **Skeletal remains**: Age (skull sutures, teeth, ossification centers), Sex (pelvis most reliable), Height (femur length)

### Age Estimation
- **Teeth**: Most reliable in children
  - Primary teeth: 6 months to 6 years
  - Permanent teeth: 6-21 years
  - Third molar eruption: 17-25 years
- **Ossification centers**: Useful 0-25 years
  - Elbow: First to fuse (14-17 years in females, 16-19 in males)
  - Iliac crest: Last to fuse (21-25 years)
- **Skull sutures**: Useful in adults — sagittal suture closes 30-40 years internally

### Sex Determination from Skeleton
- **Pelvis** (most reliable): Female — wider, shallow, round inlet, wider sub-pubic angle (>90°), wider sciatic notch
- **Skull**: Male — prominent glabella, supraorbital ridges, mastoid process, nuchal lines
- **Femur head diameter**: Male >47mm, Female <43mm

## Injuries and Wounds

### Blunt Force Injuries
- **Abrasion**: Superficial skin loss — direction indicates direction of force
  - Types: Scratch, graze, pressure, imprint
- **Contusion (Bruise)**: Extravasation of blood in tissues — color changes help time estimation
  - Red → Blue-black (1-3 days) → Green (4-7 days) → Yellow (7-14 days) → Normal (2-4 weeks)
- **Laceration**: Tearing of tissue — irregular margins, bridging vessels/nerves (vs incised wound)

### Sharp Force Injuries
- **Incised wound**: Clean-cut margins, no bridging, gaping (elastic recoil of skin)
- **Stab wound**: Depth > surface length
  - Entry wound shape indicates weapon (triangular → bayonet, slit-like → knife)
- **Chop wound**: Heavy sharp weapon (axe, sword) — incised + crushed bone

### Firearm Injuries
- **Contact wound**: Muzzle pressed against skin — stellate (star-shaped), cherry red (CO), soot in wound
- **Close range** (15 cm): Tattooing (unburnt powder grains embedded in skin — cannot be washed)
- **Intermediate range** (>60 cm): Blackening (soot — can be washed), no tattooing
- **Distant range**: Only hole, no stippling/tattooing/soot
- **Entry wound**: Inverted margins, collar of abrasion | **Exit wound**: Everted margins, larger, irregular

## Toxicology

### General Principles
- **LD50**: Dose lethal to 50% of test population
- **Antidotes**: Naloxone (opioids), Atropine+PAM (organophosphates), NAC (paracetamol), Desferrioxamine (iron), BAL (arsenic, mercury, lead), EDTA (lead)

### Common Poisons — UPSC CMS Important
| Poison | Features | Antidote |
|--------|----------|----------|
| Organophosphate | Cholinergic crisis (SLUDGE — Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI cramps, Emesis), miosis, fasiculations | Atropine + Pralidoxime (PAM) |
| Cyanide | Bitter almond smell, cherry red color, rapid death | Sodium thiosulfate, Hydroxocobalamin, Amyl nitrite |
| Arsenic | "Rice water" stools, Mees lines (nails), peripheral neuropathy, rain drop pigmentation | BAL (dimercaprol) |
| Lead | Wrist drop, basophilic stippling, lead line on gums (Burton's line), colicky pain, encephalopathy in children | EDTA (adults), DMSA (children) |
| Mercury | Acrodynia (pink disease), erethism, Minamata disease (methyl mercury — neurological) | BAL, DMSA |
| Dhatura | Anticholinergic toxidrome — mydriasis, tachycardia, dry skin, retention, delirium | Physostigmine |
| Oleander | Cardiac glycoside effects — bradycardia, heart block, hyperkalemia | Digoxin-specific antibodies |
| Copper sulfate | Blue-green vomit, hepatotoxicity, hemolysis | D-Penicillamine, BAL |
| Paracetamol | Hepatotoxicity (centrilobular necrosis), Rumack-Matthew nomogram | N-Acetylcysteine (NAC) |

### Alcohol
- **Legal level for driving** (India): 30 mg/dL (0.03%)
- **Lethal dose**: ~300-400 mg/dL
- **Methanol poisoning**: "Blindness" — formic acid metabolite damages optic nerve and retina
  - Treatment: Ethanol or fomepizole (competitive ADH inhibitor) + Dialysis

## Medical Jurisprudence

### Indian Laws Important for CMS

#### Indian Penal Code (IPC) — Now Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS)
- **Section 299/300 (IPC)**: Culpable homicide/Murder
- **Section 304A (IPC)**: Death by negligence
- **Section 304B (IPC)**: Dowry death (within 7 years of marriage)
- **Section 375/376 (IPC)**: Sexual assault/Rape
- **Section 84 (IPC)**: Act of unsound mind — McNaughten's rule (insanity defense)

#### Medical Practice Laws
- **Consumer Protection Act (CPA) 2019**: Medical negligence is a "deficiency in service"
  - Applies to private practitioners
  - Does NOT apply to free government hospital services
- **Res ipsa loquitur**: "The thing speaks for itself" — obvious negligence (wrong limb surgery, sponge left inside)
- **Bolam test**: Standard of care judged by responsible body of medical opinion
- **Therapeutic privilege**: Doctor can withhold information if disclosure would harm patient
- **Informed consent**: Written consent for invasive procedures — patient must understand nature, risks, alternatives
  - Age of consent for medical treatment: 18 years
  - Emergency: Consent not needed (implied consent)

#### Medico-Legal Cases (MLC)
- **All injury cases (assault, accident, poisoning)** must be registered as MLC  
- **Dying declaration**: Statement by person who believes death is imminent — admissible in court even without oath
  - Can be recorded by magistrate, doctor, police officer, or even layperson
- **Inquest**: Investigation into cause of death
  - Police inquest (Section 174 CrPC): Most common in India
  - Magistrate's inquest: Deaths in custody, dowry death, exhumation cases

### Medical Ethics
- **Hippocratic Oath**: "First, do no harm" (Primum non nocere)
- **Four principles**: Autonomy, Beneficence, Non-maleficence, Justice
- **Indian Medical Council (Ethics) Regulations 2002**: Code of conduct for doctors
  - Duties: Maintain confidentiality, maintain records, no euthanasia, no adultery with patients
  - Disciplinary action by State Medical Council or NMC (National Medical Commission)
- **Professional misconduct**: Dichotomy (fee-splitting), advertising, issuing false certificates, performing euthanasia, negligence

### Sexual Offenses
- **Medical examination in rape**: Within 72 hours ideally
  - Injuries: Genital and extra-genital
  - Evidence collection: Vaginal swabs for spermatozoa, DNA, clothing
  - Two-finger test: NO LONGER valid — banned by Supreme Court (2013)
- **Age of consent** (India): 18 years (both male and female)
- **Virginity status**: Not medically provable — hymen status is NOT evidence of sexual activity
- **Sexual assault evidence kit**: Chain of custody must be maintained

## Forensic Psychiatry

### Competence and Legal Capacity
- **Testamentary capacity**: Ability to make a valid will — must understand nature of act, extent of property, claims of heirs
- **Fitness to stand trial**: Ability to understand charges and participate in defense
- **McNaughten's rule**: At the time of act, person was incapable of knowing nature of act or that it was wrong

### Civil and Criminal Responsibility
- **Section 84 IPC**: Nothing is an offense if done by person of unsound mind
- **Lucid interval**: If crime committed during lucid interval → person is responsible
- **Automatism**: Involuntary act (e.g., sleepwalking) — defense against criminal liability

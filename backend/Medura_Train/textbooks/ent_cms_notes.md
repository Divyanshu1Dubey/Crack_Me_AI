# ENT (Otorhinolaryngology) — UPSC CMS Comprehensive Notes

## Ear

### Anatomy of the Ear
- **External ear**: Pinna + External auditory canal (EAC — lateral 1/3 cartilaginous, medial 2/3 bony)
- **Middle ear**: Tympanic membrane, ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes), Eustachian tube
- **Inner ear**: Cochlea (hearing), semicircular canals + vestibule (balance)
- **Tympanic membrane**: Pars tensa (main), Pars flaccida (Shrapnell's membrane — attic region)
- **Nerve supply of TM**: Auriculotemporal (V3) laterally, Arnold's nerve (vagus) medially

### Hearing Tests
- **Weber test**: Tuning fork on vertex
  - Conductive loss: Lateralizes to affected ear
  - Sensorineural loss: Lateralizes to normal ear
- **Rinne test**: Compare air vs bone conduction
  - Normal/SNHL: AC > BC (Rinne positive)
  - Conductive loss: BC > AC (Rinne negative)
- **Absolute bone conduction (ABC)**: Compares patient's BC with examiner's
- **Pure tone audiometry**: Gold standard — assesses AC and BC at different frequencies

### Otitis Media
- **Acute suppurative OM (ASOM)**: Most common organism — S. pneumoniae (children), H. influenzae
  - Stages: Hyperemia → Exudation → Suppuration → Coalescence → Resolution
  - Treatment: Amoxicillin (first-line), myringotomy if needed
- **Chronic suppurative OM (CSOM)**:
  - **Tubotympanic (safe/mucosal)**: Central perforation, mucoid discharge — conductive loss
  - **Atticoantral (unsafe/squamous)**: Marginal/attic perforation, cholesteatoma — dangerous
- **Cholesteatoma**: Keratinizing squamous epithelium in middle ear — bone erosion
  - Treatment: Mastoidectomy (canal wall down/up)
  - Complications: Intracranial abscess, lateral sinus thrombosis, facial nerve palsy

### Otosclerosis
- **Fixation of stapes footplate** by spongy bone
- **Most common cause** of conductive hearing loss in adults with intact TM
- **Bilateral** in 80%, autosomal dominant
- **Schwartze sign**: Pink reflex on otoscopy (vascular promontory)
- **Carhart's notch**: Dip at 2000 Hz on audiometry
- **Treatment**: Stapedectomy/stapedotomy, hearing aid

### Meniere's Disease
- **Endolymphatic hydrops** — episodic vertigo + tinnitus + fluctuating SNHL + aural fullness
- **Duration**: Episodes lasting 20 min to several hours
- **Low frequency** SNHL initially
- **Treatment**: Salt restriction, diuretics, betahistine, gentamicin injection (refractory)

### BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo)
- **Most common cause** of vertigo
- **Otoconia** (calcium carbonate crystals) displaced into semicircular canals (posterior most common)
- **Dix-Hallpike test**: Diagnostic — fatigable nystagmus with latency
- **Treatment**: Epley maneuver (canalith repositioning)

## Nose and Sinuses

### Nasal Polyps
- **Ethmoidal polyps**: Most common — allergic, bilateral
  - Samter's triad: Aspirin sensitivity + asthma + nasal polyps
- **Antrochoanal polyp**: Unilateral, arises from maxillary sinus → presents in choana
  - Treatment: Caldwell-Luc approach or endoscopic removal
- **Treatment**: Intranasal steroids (first-line), polypectomy for refractory cases

### Sinusitis
- **Most commonly affected sinus**: Maxillary (largest sinus)
- **Sinus most commonly affected in children**: Ethmoid
- **Complications**: Orbital cellulitis (most common complication of ethmoid sinusitis), cavernous sinus thrombosis
- **Acute sinusitis**: <4 weeks — S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, M. catarrhalis
- **Chronic sinusitis**: >12 weeks — mixed flora, anaerobes
- **Fungal sinusitis**: Aspergillus, Mucormycosis (rhinocerebral — in diabetic ketoacidosis)

### Epistaxis
- **Little's area (Kiesselbach's plexus)**: Most common site — anterior septum
  - Anastomosis of: Sphenopalatine, anterior ethmoidal, superior labial, greater palatine arteries
- **Anterior epistaxis**: 90% cases — first aid: pinch nose, lean forward
- **Posterior epistaxis**: Sphenopalatine artery — more severe, older patients
- **Management**: Anterior packing → Posterior packing → Surgical (arterial ligation/embolization)

### Deviated Nasal Septum
- **Most common nasal disorder**
- **Types**: C-shaped, S-shaped, spur, thickening
- **Symptoms**: Nasal obstruction, recurrent sinusitis, headache, epistaxis
- **Treatment**: Septoplasty (surgery of choice), SMR (submucous resection)

## Throat and Larynx

### Tonsillitis
- **Acute tonsillitis**: Group A β-hemolytic Streptococcus (GABHS)
  - Centor criteria: Fever, tonsillar exudate, tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy, absence of cough
  - Treatment: Penicillin V (first-line), Azithromycin (penicillin allergy)
  - Complications: Peritonsillar abscess (quinsy), parapharyngeal abscess, rheumatic fever, PSGN
- **Peritonsillar abscess**: Unilateral tonsillar swelling, uvula deviation to opposite side, trismus
  - Treatment: I&D + antibiotics

### Indications for Tonsillectomy
1. Recurrent tonsillitis (Paradise criteria: ≥7 episodes/year, ≥5/year for 2 years, ≥3/year for 3 years)
2. Peritonsillar abscess (second episode)
3. Tonsillar hypertrophy causing OSA
4. Suspected malignancy (unilateral tonsillar enlargement in adult)

### Laryngeal Carcinoma
- **Most common site**: Glottic (true vocal cords) — presents early with hoarseness
- **Supraglottic**: Presents late with lymph node metastasis
- **Risk factors**: Smoking, alcohol, HPV
- **Treatment**: Early — laser excision, radiotherapy | Advanced — laryngectomy + radiotherapy

### Vocal Cord Paralysis
- **Unilateral RLN palsy**: Hoarseness, cord in paramedian position
  - Causes: Thyroid surgery (most common iatrogenic), bronchogenic carcinoma, aortic aneurysm
- **Bilateral RLN palsy**: Stridor, airway compromise — may need tracheostomy
  - Cords in median position (adducted)
- **Left RLN** more commonly affected (longer course)
  - Ortner syndrome: Left RLN compression by dilated left atrium/pulmonary artery

### Tracheostomy
- **Types**: Emergency (cricothyrotomy), surgical, percutaneous
- **Site**: Between 2nd and 3rd or 3rd and 4th tracheal rings
- **Indications**: Upper airway obstruction, prolonged intubation, respiratory failure, sleep apnea
- **Complications**: Hemorrhage, pneumothorax, subcutaneous emphysema, tracheal stenosis

### Deep Neck Space Infections
- **Ludwig's angina**: Bilateral submandibular space infection — dental origin (2nd/3rd mandibular molars)
  - "Bull neck" appearance, woody induration, airway compromise
  - Treatment: IV antibiotics + airway management + surgical drainage
- **Parapharyngeal abscess**: Trismus, medial displacement of lateral pharyngeal wall
- **Retropharyngeal abscess**: Children < 5 years — dysphagia, neck stiffness, drooling

## Common ENT Emergencies

### Foreign Body
- **Nose**: Common in children — unilateral foul-smelling discharge — suspect foreign body
- **Ear**: Insects (kill with mineral oil first), beads, batteries (urgent removal)
- **Airway**: Choking — Heimlich maneuver | Back blows for infants
- **Esophagus**: Coin lodgement at cricopharynx (most common site)
  - Button batteries: Emergency removal (risk of perforation)

### Hearing Loss in Children
- **Congenital SNHL**: Rubella, CMV, ototoxic drugs, genetic (connexin 26 mutation most common)
- **Screening**: Universal newborn hearing screening (OAE/ABR)
- **Cochlear implant**: Bilateral severe-profound SNHL — best results if implanted before age 2

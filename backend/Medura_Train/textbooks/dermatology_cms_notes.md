# Dermatology — UPSC CMS Comprehensive Notes

## Basics of Skin

### Skin Layers
- **Epidermis**: Stratified squamous keratinized epithelium
  - Layers (deep to superficial): Basale → Spinosum → Granulosum → Lucidum (palms/soles) → Corneum
  - **Keratinocytes**: Main cells (90%), produce keratin
  - **Melanocytes**: Neural crest derived, in basal layer, produce melanin
  - **Langerhans cells**: Antigen-presenting cells (dendritic cells of skin)
  - **Merkel cells**: Mechanoreceptors
- **Dermis**: Collagen, elastin, blood vessels, nerves, appendages
  - Papillary dermis (superficial) and Reticular dermis (deep)
- **Hypodermis (Subcutaneous)**: Fat, blood vessels

### Skin Lesions — Terminology
- **Macule**: Flat, <1 cm, color change only | **Patch**: Flat, >1 cm
- **Papule**: Raised, solid, <1 cm | **Plaque**: Raised, flat-topped, >1 cm
- **Nodule**: Solid, >1 cm, deep | **Vesicle**: Fluid-filled, <1 cm | **Bulla**: Fluid-filled, >1 cm
- **Pustule**: Pus-filled | **Wheal**: Edematous, transient (urticaria)
- **Erosion**: Loss of epidermis | **Ulcer**: Loss of epidermis + dermis

## Bacterial Infections

### Impetigo
- **Non-bullous impetigo**: S. aureus or Group A Streptococcus — honey-colored crusts, highly contagious
- **Bullous impetigo**: S. aureus (exfoliative toxin) — flaccid blisters
- **Treatment**: Topical mupirocin (limited), oral dicloxacillin/cephalexin (extensive)
- **Complication**: Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis (not prevented by treatment)

### Leprosy (Hansen's Disease)
- **Causative agent**: Mycobacterium leprae — obligate intracellular, acid-fast, cannot be cultured
- **Ridley-Jopling Classification**:
  | Type | Skin Lesions | Nerve Involvement | Bacillary Load | Lepromin Test |
  |------|-------------|-------------------|----------------|---------------|
  | TT (Tuberculoid) | Few, well-defined, hypopigmented, anesthetic | Single/few nerves | Low (paucibacillary) | Strongly positive |
  | BT | Few, asymmetric, satellite lesions | Few nerves | Low | Positive |
  | BB (Borderline) | Many, variable morphology | Variable | Moderate | Negative |
  | BL | Many, diffuse, poorly defined | Many nerves | High | Negative |
  | LL (Lepromatous) | Numerous, symmetric, nodular, leonine facies | Bilateral, symmetric | Very high (multibacillary) | Negative |
- **WHO Classification**:
  - Paucibacillary (PB): ≤5 skin lesions, 1 nerve — MDT: Rifampicin + Dapsone × 6 months
  - Multibacillary (MB): >5 skin lesions, >1 nerve — MDT: Rifampicin + Dapsone + Clofazimine × 12 months
- **Reactions**:
  - Type 1 (Reversal): Cell-mediated, borderline types — edema of existing lesions, neuritis — Steroids
  - Type 2 (ENL — Erythema nodosum leprosum): Immune complex, LL/BL — tender nodules, fever — Thalidomide/Steroids
- **Nerve involvement**: Ulnar (most common), lateral popliteal, posterior tibial, median, facial
- **Deformities**: Claw hand, foot drop, trophic ulcers, nasal collapse

### Cutaneous Tuberculosis
- **Lupus vulgaris**: Most common form in India — apple-jelly nodules, butterfly distribution on face
- **Scrofuloderma**: TB of lymph nodes → sinus tracts
- **Treatment**: Standard ATT (anti-tubercular therapy)

## Viral Infections

### Herpes Simplex
- **HSV-1**: Orolabial herpes — cold sores, herpetic gingivostomatitis
- **HSV-2**: Genital herpes — painful vesicles on erythematous base
- **Diagnosis**: Tzanck smear (multinucleated giant cells), PCR
- **Treatment**: Oral acyclovir/valacyclovir — suppressive therapy for frequent recurrences

### Herpes Zoster (Shingles)
- **Reactivation of VZV** in dorsal root ganglion — painful vesicles in dermatomal distribution
- **Does not cross midline** — unilateral
- **Herpes zoster ophthalmicus**: V1 involvement — keratitis, iritis (Hutchinson sign: nose tip vesicles)
- **Post-herpetic neuralgia**: Pain persisting >3 months — treated with gabapentin, pregabalin
- **Treatment**: Acyclovir/Valacyclovir within 72 hours of onset

### Warts (Verruca)
- **Caused by HPV** (Human Papillomavirus)
- **Common warts**: HPV 2, 4 — hands, fingers
- **Plantar warts**: HPV 1 — soles, painful on walking
- **Genital warts (Condylomata acuminata)**: HPV 6, 11 — cauliflower-like
- **Cervical cancer**: HPV 16, 18
- **Treatment**: Cryotherapy, salicylic acid, podophyllin (genital), imiquimod

### Molluscum Contagiosum
- **Pox virus** — dome-shaped, umbilicated, pearly papules
- **Diagnosis**: Henderson-Patterson bodies (eosinophilic intracytoplasmic inclusions)
- **Treatment**: Self-limiting in immunocompetent, curettage, cryotherapy

## Fungal Infections

### Dermatophytosis (Tinea/Ringworm)
- **Organisms**: Trichophyton, Microsporum, Epidermophyton — invade keratinized tissue only
- **Tinea corporis**: Annular, scaly patch with central clearing — "ringworm"
- **Tinea capitis**: Scalp — patchy alopecia with broken hairs, grey patch, kerion (inflammatory)
- **Tinea pedis (Athlete's foot)**: Most common dermatophytosis worldwide
- **Tinea cruris (Jock itch)**: Inner thigh, sparing scrotum
- **Tinea unguium (Onychomycosis)**: Distal subungual most common — thickened, yellowed nails
- **Diagnosis**: KOH mount (hyphae), fungal culture (Sabouraud dextrose agar)
- **Treatment**: Topical (clotrimazole, terbinafine) for limited; Oral (terbinafine, griseofulvin) for extensive/nails

### Pityriasis Versicolor
- **Malassezia furfur** (lipophilic yeast)
- **Hypo/hyperpigmented** scaly macules on trunk, "rain drop" pattern
- **KOH mount**: "Spaghetti and meatball" appearance
- **Treatment**: Topical ketoconazole, selenium sulfide shampoo

### Candidiasis
- **Candida albicans** — oral thrush, vaginal candidiasis, diaper dermatitis, intertrigo
- **Oral thrush**: White, curd-like plaques on erythematous base — common in HIV, denture wearers
- **Chronic mucocutaneous candidiasis**: Rare, T-cell defect
- **Treatment**: Topical nystatin/clotrimazole, oral fluconazole (systemic)

## Autoimmune Skin Diseases

### Pemphigus
- **Pemphigus vulgaris** (most common and most severe):
  - **Intraepidermal** blisters (suprabasal split) — flaccid, rupture easily → erosions
  - **Nikolsky sign**: Positive (sliding pressure separates epidermis)
  - **Autoantibodies**: IgG against Desmoglein 3 (mucosal) and Desmoglein 1 (cutaneous)
  - **Histology**: Acantholysis (loss of cell-cell adhesion)
  - **DIF (Direct immunofluorescence)**: "Fishnet/chicken wire" pattern — IgG intercellular
  - **Treatment**: Systemic steroids + steroid-sparing agent (azathioprine, mycophenolate), rituximab

### Bullous Pemphigoid
- **Subepidermal** blisters — tense, don't rupture easily
- **Nikolsky sign**: Negative
- **Autoantibodies**: IgG against BP180/BP230 (hemidesmosomes — dermoepidermal junction)
- **DIF**: Linear IgG and C3 at BMZ (basement membrane zone)
- **Elderly** patients, pruritic
- **Treatment**: Topical/systemic steroids, doxycycline

### Dermatitis Herpetiformis
- **Intensely pruritic** grouped vesicles on extensor surfaces (elbows, knees, buttocks)
- **Associated with Celiac disease** (even if asymptomatic)
- **DIF**: Granular IgA at dermal papillae
- **Treatment**: Dapsone (dramatic response), gluten-free diet

## Psoriasis
- **Chronic, immune-mediated** disease — epidermal hyperproliferation
- **Silvery-white scales** on well-defined erythematous plaques
- **Sites**: Extensor surfaces (elbows, knees), scalp, sacrum, nails
- **Auspitz sign**: Pin-point bleeding on removing scale (dilated dermal capillaries)
- **Koebner phenomenon**: Lesions at sites of trauma/injury
- **Nail changes**: Pitting, oil drop sign, onycholysis, subungual hyperkeratosis
- **Psoriatic arthritis**: Sausage digits (dactylitis), DIP involvement, pencil-in-cup deformity

### Types
- **Chronic plaque** (most common, 90%): Thick, silvery scales
- **Guttate**: Drop-like lesions, post-streptococcal infection, young adults
- **Pustular**: Sterile pustules — von Zumbusch (generalized, life-threatening)
- **Erythrodermic**: >90% BSA involvement — medical emergency
- **Inverse**: Flexural areas — shiny, red, no scaling

### Treatment
- **Mild**: Topical steroids, vitamin D analogs (calcipotriol), tar preparations
- **Moderate**: Phototherapy (NB-UVB, PUVA)
- **Severe**: Methotrexate (first-line systemic), cyclosporine, acitretin
- **Biologics**: Anti-TNF (adalimumab), Anti-IL-17 (secukinumab), Anti-IL-23 (guselkumab)

## Eczema/Dermatitis

### Atopic Dermatitis
- **Most common chronic skin disease** in children
- **Atopic triad**: Eczema + asthma + allergic rhinitis
- **Age-related distribution**:
  - Infants: Face, scalp, extensor surfaces
  - Children: Flexural areas (antecubital, popliteal fossa)
  - Adults: Hands, face, flexures
- **Features**: Intense pruritus, dry skin, lichenification (chronic rubbing)
- **Elevated IgE**
- **Treatment**: Emollients (mainstay), topical steroids, calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus)

### Contact Dermatitis
- **Irritant**: Non-immune, from direct skin damage (detergents, acids) — most common
- **Allergic**: Type IV hypersensitivity — nickel (most common allergen), rubber, cosmetics
  - **Diagnosis**: Patch testing (gold standard)
- **Treatment**: Avoid allergen, topical steroids, emollients

## Skin Tumors

### Melanoma
- **Most lethal skin cancer** (though less common than BCC/SCC)
- **Risk factors**: UV exposure, fair skin, dysplastic nevi, family history, CDKN2A mutation
- **ABCDE criteria**: Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Color variation, Diameter >6mm, Evolving
- **Types**: Superficial spreading (most common), Nodular (worst prognosis), Lentigo maligna, Acral lentiginous (most common in dark-skinned)
- **Staging**: Breslow thickness (most important prognostic factor)
- **Treatment**: Wide excision, sentinel lymph node biopsy, immunotherapy (nivolumab, pembrolizumab)

### Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC)
- **Most common skin cancer** overall
- **Rodent ulcer**: Pearly, translucent nodule with rolled borders, telangiectasia, central ulceration
- **Locally invasive**, rarely metastasizes
- **Sun-exposed areas** (face — especially nose)
- **Treatment**: Surgical excision, Mohs surgery (for high-risk areas), radiotherapy

### Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC)
- **Second most common** skin cancer
- **Risk**: UV, immunosuppression, chronic wounds (Marjolin's ulcer), arsenic exposure
- **Can metastasize** (unlike BCC)
- **Treatment**: Excision, radiation for inoperable

## Drug Reactions

### Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) / Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN)
- **SJS**: <10% BSA detachment | **SJS-TEN overlap**: 10-30% | **TEN**: >30% BSA
- **Common drugs**: Sulfonamides, carbamazepine, allopurinol, NSAIDs, phenytoin
- **Nikolsky sign positive** — full thickness epidermal necrosis
- **Treatment**: Stop offending drug, supportive care (burn unit), IV Ig, cyclosporine
- **SCORTEN**: Severity scoring system

### Drug Rash Patterns
- **Morbilliform** (most common drug rash): Diffuse maculopapular — ampicillin, allopurinol
- **Urticaria**: Penicillin, NSAIDs
- **Fixed drug eruption**: Same site each time — tetracyclines, co-trimoxazole, NSAIDs
- **DRESS syndrome**: Drug rash with eosinophilia and systemic symptoms — anticonvulsants, allopurinol
- **Photosensitivity**: Tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones, amiodarone
